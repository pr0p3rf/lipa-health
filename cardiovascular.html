The pattern we see every day

"My cholesterol is great. Why is my dad on his second heart attack?"

You're 40, 45, 50. Your annual physical comes back. Total cholesterol 180. LDL 110. HDL 45. Your doctor says "everything looks fine, see you next year."

What your doctor didn't run: ApoB. Lp(a). hs-CRP. Those are the numbers that actually predict heart attacks — and standard panels don't include them. The 2019 Mendelian randomization study by Ference et al. settled the ApoB-vs-LDL question: ApoB is the better predictor when the two diverge, and they diverge in roughly 30% of patients. Lp(a) is set at birth and affects ~20% of adults. Most people have never been tested.

Total cholesterol is the cardiovascular equivalent of measuring your weight without measuring your body fat. The number tells you something. It doesn't tell you what.

If your father had a heart attack at 55 with "normal cholesterol," there's a high probability he had elevated Lp(a) that no one tested. You inherited 50% of his Lp(a). Lipa flags this on every cardiovascular panel.

Your basic panel reads "normal." It's not. ApoB at 112 means you have a high atherogenic particle count even though LDL-C is 108 — classic ApoB-LDL discordance, and ApoB wins the predictive contest. Common questions.

Why is ApoB more important than LDL cholesterol?
LDL cholesterol measures cholesterol carried by LDL particles. ApoB measures the number of atherogenic particles directly. The 2019 Mendelian randomization study by Ference et al. shows ApoB is a stronger predictor of cardiovascular events, especially when the two are discordant (~30% of patients). ApoB above 90 mg/dL warrants attention; above 110 is high risk regardless of LDL-C.
What is Lp(a) and why does it matter?
Lipoprotein(a) is an LDL-like particle with apolipoprotein(a) attached. It's the single largest genetic CV risk factor — affects ~20% of adults. Levels are set largely at birth, don't change with diet, exercise, or statins. AHA's March 2026 guidelines now recommend universal Lp(a) screening at least once per lifetime. Most people have never been tested.
What is the triglyceride-to-HDL ratio?
A well-validated proxy for insulin resistance and atherogenic dyslipidemia. Above 3.5 is concerning; above 5 strongly suggests insulin resistance and small-dense LDL. Often catches metabolic dysfunction earlier than fasting glucose or A1c.
What is the role of hs-CRP in cardiovascular risk?
hs-CRP is a marker of vascular inflammation. JUPITER trial (NEJM 2008) showed people with normal LDL but elevated hs-CRP had significant CV event reduction with statin therapy — meaning inflammation is an independent risk axis. Above 2 = high-risk; under 1 = optimal.
Should I test homocysteine?
Homocysteine elevation is associated with CV events, dementia risk, and methylation problems. Above 10 µmol/L often signals B12, B6, or folate insufficiency, or MTHFR variants. Lipa connects it to specific methylated B-complex recommendations.

The pattern we see every day

"My doctor said the labs look fine. So why am I losing my hair?"

You're four months into Wegovy. Down 8 kilos. Sleep is better. Energy is up. But your hair is thinning, your gym performance is plateauing, and you're more tired in the afternoon than you used to be.

Your prescriber says everything looks normal. They probably ran A1c, basic metabolic panel, and a lipid panel. They probably didn't run lipase, full thyroid, vitamin B12, or do a deliberate look at creatinine velocity. The problems GLP-1 users actually run into don't show up in the basic monitoring — but they do show up in the bloodwork, if someone reads it carefully.

GLP-1 reduces appetite. Reduced appetite reduces protein intake, which reduces muscle mass. It also reduces B12 ingestion. Both show on bloodwork as patterns most prescribers don't look for. Hair thinning at month 4 is rarely about the medication — it's almost always nutrient depletion the medication caused.

Lipa flags the patterns. Every panel. Every retest.

Lipa's GLP-1 interpretation
The headline numbers are excellent: A1c down to 5.5%, ALT improving (NAFLD reversing — the cardiovascular and liver health win you took the medication for). But three signals need attention. Lipase at 2.5× your baseline (25 → 62) is in pancreatitis-watch territory even though the absolute value is barely above the lab cutoff — rate of change is what matters. Creatinine has fallen from 0.92 to 0.74 while you've lost 8kg; that's the muscle-loss signal we watch for, and your eGFR drop reflects this rather than kidney damage. B12 at 340 and ferritin halved — this is your hair. Protocol: methylcobalamin 1000mcg daily, iron bisglycinate 25mg + vitamin C with breakfast (separate from any thyroid med by 4hrs), prioritize 1.6g/kg protein daily and resistance training 3×/week to defend muscle, hydrate aggressively, and retest lipase + creatinine + B12 in 8 weeks. If lipase climbs above 75 or you develop epigastric pain, stop the medication and call your prescriber.

Common questions.

What bloodwork should I monitor on GLP-1?
Pancreatic enzymes (lipase, amylase) for pancreatitis risk, eGFR + creatinine for kidney function, TSH for thyroid surveillance (FDA Black Box), A1c + fasting glucose for diabetes response, lipid panel + ApoB for cardiovascular improvement, vitamin B12 (depleted by reduced food intake), electrolytes if you've had GI side effects, and ALT/AST for NAFLD reversal.
Why is lipase important on GLP-1?
GLP-1 medications carry a small but real pancreatitis risk. A 2-3× rise in lipase from baseline — even within reference range — warrants clinical attention. Most clinicians only flag values above 3× the upper limit of normal. Lipa flags rate of change relative to your baseline.
What is the GLP-1 thyroid black box warning?
Based on rodent studies showing increased medullary thyroid C-cell tumors. The human signal is weak but present. Standard practice: avoid GLP-1 in people with personal or family history of MEN2 syndrome or medullary thyroid carcinoma. Beyond that, baseline TSH and any palpable thyroid changes warrant investigation.
Why does GLP-1 cause B12 depletion?
Reduced food intake on GLP-1 means reduced B12 ingestion. Slowed gastric emptying may also impair absorption. Hair thinning, fatigue, and brain fog on GLP-1 are often misattributed to weight loss when they're B12 depletion. Lipa flags B12 below 500 pg/mL as actionable on GLP-1 even when conventional cutoffs say "normal."
Are muscle loss markers visible on bloodwork?
Partially. Creatinine declines as muscle mass falls. Urea drops with reduced protein intake. Lipa flags the creatinine-trending-down + urea-low pattern as a muscle loss signal, prompting protein intake review and resistance training emphasis.

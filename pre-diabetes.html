Common questions.

Is A1c at 5.7% really pre-diabetic?
Yes. CDC defines pre-diabetes as A1c 5.7-6.4%. ADA uses the same range. Above 5.6%, your 5-year risk of progressing to type 2 diabetes is roughly 25-50% without intervention. The good news: pre-diabetes is reversible at this stage. The DPP trial showed 58% diabetes risk reduction with lifestyle intervention.
Why is fasting insulin more useful than fasting glucose?
Fasting insulin rises long before fasting glucose. By the time glucose breaks 100 mg/dL, insulin has often been climbing for years. A fasting insulin above 10 µIU/mL with normal glucose is the earliest detectable signal of insulin resistance.
What is NAFLD and why does ALT matter?
Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) co-occurs with pre-diabetes in 60-80% of cases. Mildly elevated ALT (above 25 U/L for women, 30 U/L for men) often signals NAFLD before any imaging. NAFLD reverses with insulin resistance.
What is HOMA-IR?
HOMA-IR (Homeostasis Model Assessment of Insulin Resistance) is calculated as (fasting insulin × fasting glucose) / 405. Below 1 = optimal; above 2 = IR; above 3 = significant.
Can I reverse pre-diabetes?
Yes — pre-diabetes is one of the most reversible conditions in medicine when caught early. Standard interventions (Mediterranean or low-carb eating, exercise, 5-7% weight loss) drop A1c by 0.3-0.6% in 12 weeks.

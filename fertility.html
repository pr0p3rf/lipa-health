The pattern we see every day

"They told me my AMH was low. They didn't tell me what to do about it."

You're 34, 36, 39. You've been trying to conceive for 6, 9, 12 months. Or you're considering egg freezing. Or you're being workup'd for IVF. The clinic runs AMH and you get a number that gets characterized as "below age expectation." The conversation pivots to IVF protocols, donor eggs, success rates.

What's missing from the conversation: the 90-day window before each retrieval is when oocytes finalize their maturation. Egg quality at the moment of retrieval reflects mitochondrial health, oxidative load, and nutrient sufficiency over those 90 days. Vitamin D, CoQ10, omega-3, ferritin, thyroid status, glucose control — these are all modifiable. They aren't standard of care. They're standard of optimal care.

AMH tells you the size of your reserve. It doesn't tell you the quality of any individual egg. The quality you can influence in 90 days. The quantity you can't. Most clinics talk about the quantity. Lipa interprets both — and surfaces the things you can actually do.

Lipa's fertility interpretation
AMH 1.1 confirms reduced ovarian reserve relative to age 35 — but you are ovulating (progesterone 8.2 day 21) and your basic axis is intact. Four modifiable factors are working against you that no one has addressed: vitamin D 22 ng/mL is deficient (multiple meta-analyses show vitamin D >40 ng/mL improves IVF outcomes), TSH 2.6 + TPO antibodies 42 is the early Hashimoto's pattern that ASRM recommends targeting below 2.5 for fertility (some endocrinologists target below 2.0), ferritin 26 is functionally low (iron is needed for thyroid synthesis and has independent fertility effects), and prolactin 18 is borderline elevated (often a stress + thyroid finding; can suppress ovulation if it climbs higher). 90-day pre-retrieval optimization plan: vitamin D3 5000 IU + K2 MK-7 daily for 12 weeks then retest, levothyroxine consideration with your endocrinologist (TSH 2.6 + TPO+ likely warrants treatment for fertility), iron bisglycinate 25mg + vitamin C alternate-day mornings, CoQ10 (ubiquinol form) 200-400mg/day for mitochondrial support of oocytes, omega-3 EPA+DHA 2g daily, NAC 600mg twice daily, and stress-modulation work for the prolactin signal. Retest at 12 weeks. None of this changes AMH (that's structural) but it materially shifts egg quality and ovulation likelihood. Talk to your reproductive endocrinologist about treating subclinical hypothyroidism before any retrieval cycle.

Common questions.

What does AMH actually tell me?
AMH reflects the size of your ovarian reserve — how many eggs are still available. It's a quantity marker, not quality. AMH does NOT tell you your monthly chance of conception. It does predict response to ovarian stimulation if you're considering IVF.
What's the complete fertility panel?
Day 3: FSH, LH, estradiol, prolactin, AMH, TSH, fT3, fT4, TPO antibodies. Day 21: progesterone. Plus metabolic + nutrient context: vitamin D, ferritin, B12, A1c, fasting insulin, hs-CRP. Most reproductive endocrinology workups skip the metabolic and nutrient layer.
What modifiable factors affect egg quality?
Egg quality is influenced by mitochondrial function, oxidative stress, and nutrient sufficiency in the 90-100 days before ovulation. Modifiable: vitamin D status, CoQ10, inositol (PCOS), DHEA in DOR + low DHEA-S, omega-3 EPA+DHA, and reducing oxidative load.
My doctor says my AMH is too low for IVF. Is that right?
Sometimes — but second opinions matter, and modifiable factors can shift the picture. Some clinics decline to treat at AMH below 1.0; others (mini-IVF, natural-cycle IVF) will. Optimizing vitamin D, ferritin, thyroid, and considering CoQ10/DHEA for 90 days before retrieval is worth doing.
Should I freeze eggs based on my AMH?
It's a personal decision but bloodwork informs it. AMH below age-appropriate ranges, especially with rising FSH, suggests reserve is declining faster than average. Egg freezing in your 30s preserves quality.

Chapter 1

What are research peptides?

Peptides are short chains of amino acids, typically between 2 and 50 amino acids in length. They are the building blocks that form larger proteins. Unlike full proteins, which can contain hundreds or thousands of amino acids folded into complex structures, peptides are smaller, more targeted molecules that researchers study for their specific biological signaling properties.

Research peptides are synthesized in laboratories using a process called solid-phase peptide synthesis (SPPS), first developed by Robert Bruce Merrifield in 1963 (which earned him the Nobel Prize in Chemistry). This process builds the peptide chain one amino acid at a time on a solid resin support, allowing for precise control over the sequence and high levels of purity when performed correctly.

These compounds are sold strictly as research chemicals — for laboratory and in-vitro research purposes only. They are not approved for human consumption, medical treatment, or as food additives. This "for research purposes only" framework exists because while published literature shows promising biological activity for many peptides, they have not undergone the full regulatory approval process required for therapeutic use in most jurisdictions.

The research peptide market serves universities, independent laboratories, and qualified researchers who study these compounds in controlled settings. Published studies across peer-reviewed journals continue to expand our understanding of how specific peptide sequences interact with biological systems, making this an active and growing field of scientific inquiry.

Chapter 2

How to evaluate purity

Purity is the single most important factor when selecting research peptides. An impure compound can compromise experimental results, introduce confounding variables, and render an entire research project unreliable. Here is how to evaluate what you are purchasing.

📊

HPLC Testing

High-Performance Liquid Chromatography separates a sample into its components, revealing the percentage of the target peptide vs. impurities. Look for results above 98%.

🔬

Mass Spectrometry

Confirms the molecular weight of the peptide matches the expected value. This verifies the compound is actually what the label claims it to be.

A Certificate of Analysis (COA) is a document that accompanies each batch and reports the results of these tests. A legitimate COA includes the batch number, test date, testing method (HPLC and/or MS), measured purity percentage, and the identity of the testing laboratory.

However, not all COAs are created equal. Manufacturer-provided COAs — where the company tests its own product — carry an inherent conflict of interest. The most trustworthy approach is independent third-party testing. In the peptide research community, Janoshik Analytical is widely regarded as the gold standard for independent verification. They have no financial relationship with the companies whose products they test, which eliminates bias.

How to spot a questionable COA: Watch for COAs missing batch numbers, lacking a named testing laboratory, showing suspiciously round purity numbers (exactly 99.0%), or using generic templates that could be easily fabricated. A credible COA will always reference specific instrumentation and methodology.

Chapter 3

Setting up your research workspace

Proper laboratory preparation is essential for accurate and reproducible results. Before beginning any peptide research, ensure your workspace is equipped with the following supplies.

  • 💧

    Bacteriostatic Water (BAC Water)

    Sterile water containing 0.9% benzyl alcohol, which inhibits bacterial growth. Used to reconstitute lyophilized (freeze-dried) peptides into solution. The benzyl alcohol preservative allows for multiple draws from the same vial while maintaining sterility over time.

  • 💉

    Precision Dispensing Syringes

    Precision-graduated syringes (typically 1mL / 100 unit) that allow for accurate measurement of small volumes. The fine graduations are critical for precise volumetric dispensing in research settings where microgram-level accuracy matters.

  • 🧫

    Alcohol Swabs

    Pre-saturated 70% isopropyl alcohol pads used to sterilize vial stoppers before each draw. This prevents microbial contamination of the reconstituted peptide solution — a basic but essential laboratory hygiene step.

  • 🗑️

    Sharps Container

    A puncture-resistant, clearly labeled container for the safe disposal of used syringes and needles. Proper sharps disposal is a regulatory requirement in most jurisdictions and a fundamental aspect of responsible laboratory practice.

  • 🧤

    Nitrile Gloves

    Protect both the researcher and the sample. Nitrile is preferred over latex for its chemical resistance and hypoallergenic properties. Gloves prevent skin oils and contaminants from compromising the peptide sample during handling.

Chapter 4

Understanding research protocols

A large body of published scientific literature describes how various peptides have been studied in both in-vitro and in-vivo research models. Understanding how to read and interpret these protocols is an important skill for any researcher working with peptides.

Dosing in the literature is typically reported in micrograms (mcg) or milligrams (mg) per kilogram of body weight. Published studies describe wide-ranging protocols depending on the peptide being investigated, the research model used, and the specific endpoints being measured. It is important to note that dosages described in animal research do not translate directly to other contexts — allometric scaling and species-specific pharmacokinetics must be considered.

Cycle lengths referenced in studies vary significantly. Some published protocols describe short observation periods of days, while others follow subjects over weeks or months. The duration typically depends on the biological half-life of the peptide being studied and the timeframe required to observe the endpoints of interest. Researchers commonly review multiple studies to understand the range of protocols that have been explored in the literature.

Reconstitution basics: Most research peptides arrive as a lyophilized (freeze-dried) powder. Published protocols describe reconstitution by slowly adding bacteriostatic water along the wall of the vial, then gently swirling — never shaking — to dissolve the peptide. Aggressive mixing can damage peptide bonds and reduce efficacy. The volume of BAC water used determines the concentration of the final solution.

We strongly recommend that researchers familiarize themselves with the peer-reviewed literature relevant to any peptide they intend to study. Resources such as PubMed, Google Scholar, and institutional library databases provide access to published research protocols and findings.

Chapter 5

Storage and handling

Proper storage is critical to maintaining the integrity of research peptides. Degradation from heat, light, or moisture can alter the compound and compromise experimental results.

Form Temperature Shelf Life Notes
Lyophilized (powder) -20°C ideal, 2-8°C acceptable 24+ months Most stable form. Keep sealed and away from moisture.
Reconstituted (solution) 2-8°C (refrigerator) 4-6 weeks Use bacteriostatic water. Do not freeze reconstituted peptides.
Long-term archive -20°C (freezer) 36+ months Lyophilized only. Minimize freeze-thaw cycles.

Lyophilized peptides are the most stable form. The freeze-drying process removes water, which is the primary driver of degradation. In this powder form, peptides can remain stable for years when stored properly in a freezer. Keep vials sealed in their original packaging until ready for use, and store them away from direct light.

Reconstituted peptides have a shorter shelf life because they are now in solution, which reintroduces the potential for hydrolysis (breakdown by water) and microbial contamination. The bacteriostatic water slows bacterial growth, but the peptide bonds themselves will slowly degrade at refrigerator temperatures over weeks. Published protocols generally recommend using reconstituted peptides within 4 to 6 weeks.

Avoid repeated freeze-thaw cycles. Each cycle of freezing and thawing can cause physical stress on peptide structures. If you need to store reconstituted peptide long-term, the literature suggests aliquoting the solution into smaller volumes so that each portion is only thawed once.

Shipping note: Lyophilized peptides are stable at room temperature for short transit periods. Published stability data shows that most lyophilized peptides maintain their integrity for several days at ambient temperature, which is why they can be shipped safely without cold-chain logistics in most conditions.

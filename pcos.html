The pattern we see every day

"I've had PCOS for years. Why do I still feel like I'm guessing?"

PCOS affects 5-21% of women of reproductive age. It's the most common cause of anovulatory infertility. It's $8 billion of US healthcare spend a year. And it's diagnosed under criteria — Rotterdam — that group at least four distinct phenotypes under one label.

Standard care treats them all the same. Metformin if you're trying to conceive. Birth control if you're not. Maybe spironolactone for the acne. The treatment doesn't ask which version of PCOS you actually have.

If your protocol doesn't match your phenotype, you're treating the wrong thing. Insulin-resistant PCOS responds to metformin and inositol. Adrenal PCOS responds to stress modulation and DHEA management. Post-pill PCOS often resolves on its own with targeted nutrient support. Inflammatory PCOS needs the inflammation treated first.

The four phenotypes

Which one are you?

Phenotype is determined by the pattern across your bloodwork — not by symptoms alone. Lipa identifies the dominant phenotype and notes overlap when two are co-active.

Phenotype · 1 (~70%)

Insulin-resistant PCOS

The most common. Insulin signaling drives androgen overproduction. Often accompanied by central weight gain and skin tags. Carbohydrate-sensitive.

Fasting insulin >10 · HOMA-IR >2 · low SHBG · elevated free T · A1c borderline
Phenotype · 2

Inflammatory PCOS

Chronic inflammation drives the syndrome. Often co-occurs with autoimmune conditions, gut dysfunction, or chronic stress. May not show typical insulin pattern.

hs-CRP >2 · normal-borderline insulin · elevated free T · low SHBG (variable)
Phenotype · 3

Post-pill PCOS

Symptoms emerge after stopping hormonal birth control. High LH/FSH ratio, transient androgen surge. Often resolves in 6-18 months with targeted support.

LH/FSH ratio >2 · elevated androgens · normal insulin · history of recent OCP discontinuation
Phenotype · 4

Adrenal PCOS

DHEA-S is elevated as the primary androgen source rather than ovarian testosterone. Often triggered or worsened by chronic stress. Responds to stress modulation.

DHEA-S elevated · normal-low ovarian androgens · cortisol pattern often dysregulated · normal insulin

Phenotype isn't a symptom checklist — it's a pattern across fasting insulin, HOMA-IR, SHBG, free T, DHEA-S, hs-CRP, and AMH. Lipa identifies the dominant phenotype and flags overlap. The protocol changes based on the answer.

Pattern · 2

"Normal" insulin that's already insulin resistant

Fasting insulin reference ranges go up to 25 µIU/mL. But functionally, anything above 10 is insulin-resistant in PCOS context. We use PCOS-specific cutoffs, not lab cutoffs.

Pattern · 3

Suppressed SHBG as the early warning

Low SHBG can show up before insulin or A1c look abnormal. It's the most sensitive early marker of insulin signaling problems in PCOS — often suppressed by 30-50% before fasting glucose moves.

Pattern · 4

Inositol vs metformin guidance

Both work. Inositol (myo + D-chiro 40:1) matches metformin on insulin sensitivity and ovulation rate with fewer GI side effects in most studies. Lipa flags which to consider based on insulin severity, GI tolerance, and your goals.

Pattern · 5

TTC overlay protocol

If you're trying to conceive, the protocol shifts. Lipa surfaces ovulation-supporting markers (vitamin D, thyroid optimization, inositol, weight stabilization) and flags retest timing aligned to your cycle.

Pattern · 6

The thyroid-PCOS overlap

Hashimoto's is 3× more common in PCOS. Subclinical hypothyroidism worsens insulin resistance. Lipa screens TSH, fT3, fT4, and TPO antibodies on every PCOS panel — the connection most endocrinologists miss.

Common questions.

Why doesn't my doctor run more PCOS markers?
Standard PCOS workup checks total testosterone, possibly LH/FSH ratio, and ultrasound. That's enough for diagnosis under Rotterdam criteria — but not enough to identify which of the four PCOS phenotypes you have, and therefore not enough to choose a protocol. Phenotype identification requires fasting insulin, HOMA-IR, SHBG, free testosterone, DHEA-S, prolactin, hs-CRP, and AMH. Most doctors don't run these because PCOS is often treated as one disease — even though it's at least four.
What are the 4 PCOS phenotypes?
1) Insulin-resistant — elevated fasting insulin, HOMA-IR > 2, low SHBG. The most common (~70%). 2) Inflammatory — elevated hs-CRP, often co-occurs with autoimmune conditions or chronic stress. 3) Post-pill — symptoms emerge after stopping hormonal birth control, with high LH/FSH ratio. Often resolves in 6-12 months. 4) Adrenal — elevated DHEA-S as the primary androgen source, often triggered by chronic stress. Each phenotype responds to different protocols.
What's the difference between metformin and inositol?
Metformin is the standard prescribed insulin sensitizer; inositol (typically myo-inositol + D-chiro-inositol in 40:1 ratio) is the most-studied natural alternative. Multiple meta-analyses show inositol matches or exceeds metformin's effect on insulin sensitivity, ovulation rate, and pregnancy outcomes — with fewer GI side effects. The choice depends on insulin severity, GI tolerance, and physician guidance. Lipa flags the protocol best matched to your phenotype and bloodwork patterns.
Why is SHBG important in PCOS?
SHBG (sex hormone binding globulin) binds testosterone and keeps it inactive. Low SHBG means more free testosterone is biologically active — driving acne, hair loss, and irregular cycles. SHBG is suppressed by insulin, so low SHBG is a marker of insulin resistance even when fasting glucose looks normal. SHBG above 60 nmol/L is generally protective; below 35 indicates active androgen excess and insulin signaling problems.
Can Lipa help if I'm trying to conceive?
Yes. PCOS is the most common cause of anovulatory infertility, and bloodwork interpretation matters even more when TTC. Lipa interprets AMH, FSH, LH, estradiol, progesterone, prolactin, TSH, and vitamin D in the fertility context — flagging which interventions improve ovulation likelihood (inositol, vitamin D repletion, weight stabilization, thyroid optimization) and which retest cadence makes sense for tracking response. We are not a fertility clinic and don't replace one — but we make sure you walk in armed with a complete picture.

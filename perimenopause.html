The pattern we see every day

"Why does everyone keep telling me my labs are fine?"

You're 44, 47, 51. Sleep cratered. Anxiety arrived for the first time in your life. Weight redistributed even though you didn't change anything. Brain fog. Joint pain. Libido gone. You ask your doctor for hormone testing and she tells you the values are "too variable to be useful."

That's the standard line — and it dismisses millions of women who feel different and want data.

Hormones fluctuate. So do trajectories. Tracked over 3-6 months and combined with the rest of the panel — lipid shifts, thyroid status, vitamin D, DHEA-S — bloodwork becomes the most informative tool you have. The "unnecessary" position assumes a single FSH value is the only thing being measured. It isn't.

Lipa interprets the full picture, including the trajectories. And in perimenopause, the rest of the body is shifting too — ApoB rises as estrogen falls, insulin sensitivity declines, bone density starts to drop. Most providers don't connect these dots to perimenopause. We do.

Lipa's stage call + HRT context
Mid-to-late menopause transition per STRAW+10. FSH 22 + estradiol 38 + 35-65 day cycles place you here. Anovulatory progesterone (2.1) is consistent — and explains the sleep disruption (progesterone is sedating; without it, sleep architecture deteriorates first). Free testosterone is low; DHEA-S is at the bottom of range — both common in transition and contributing to libido and energy decline. Cardiovascular shift is already happening: ApoB 112 is elevated, Lp(a) 45 borderline. Estrogen's lipid-protective effect is fading; this is the window where proactive cardio risk management matters most. Vitamin D 22 is deficient (autoimmune-threshold cutoff is 40), ferritin 28 is functionally low even if "in range," and A1c 5.5 is at the pre-diabetic edge. HRT context: you're a strong candidate for transdermal estradiol (lower clot risk than oral) + cyclical micronized progesterone, with the cardiovascular profile arguing for earlier rather than later initiation. Independently, the protocol leads with vitamin D3 5000 IU + K2 MK-7, iron bisglycinate 25mg + vitamin C, magnesium glycinate 400mg before bed, resistance training 3×/week (bone density), and a low-glycemic eating pattern. Retest at 12 weeks.

The progesterone-sleep connection

Progesterone is sedating. As ovulation becomes irregular, progesterone drops, and sleep architecture is the first thing to break. Lipa connects luteal progesterone to your sleep complaint and flags cyclical micronized progesterone as a candidate intervention.

Pattern · 5

Bone-health early signals

Bone density loss accelerates in late perimenopause. Lipa flags vitamin D, K2, calcium, DHEA-S, and the metabolic markers (A1c, hsCRP) that predict bone turnover risk — years before a DEXA would.

Pattern · 6

The thyroid-perimenopause overlap

Hashimoto's prevalence rises sharply in midlife women, and thyroid symptoms (fatigue, weight, brain fog) are easy to misattribute to perimenopause. Lipa screens TSH, fT3, fT4, and TPO antibodies on every panel to rule it in or out.

Common questions.

Why does my doctor say hormone testing in perimenopause is unnecessary?
ACOG and most endocrinologists hold that hormones fluctuate so much during perimenopause that single-point testing is "meaningless." That's true if you only test once and only test FSH. But trended over 3-6 months — and combined with symptom tracking, lipid changes, thyroid status, vitamin D, and DHEA-S — bloodwork becomes the most informative tool you have for staging and HRT decisions. The "unnecessary" position dismisses millions of women who feel different and want data. Lipa interprets the full picture across time.
What's the complete perimenopause panel?
FSH, LH, estradiol, progesterone (cycle-day-aware), total + free testosterone, SHBG, DHEA-S, TSH, free T3, free T4, TPO antibodies, vitamin D (25-OH), ferritin, fasting insulin, fasting glucose, A1c, hs-CRP, lipid panel including ApoB and Lp(a). Most physicians run a small subset. Lipa interprets all of these together, against perimenopause-specific reference points.
Why does Lipa flag ApoB and Lp(a) in perimenopause?
Estrogen is cardioprotective. As estradiol drops in perimenopause, ApoB rises, LDL particle number increases, and the lifetime cardiovascular risk profile changes. Lp(a) — set largely at birth but unmasked clinically when estrogen falls — becomes a more visible risk in this window. Most cardiologists don't connect this to perimenopause; most gynecologists don't think to track lipids. Lipa does both.
What is the HRT-decision panel?
If you're considering HRT, the relevant data is broader than estradiol. Lipa surfaces estradiol/FSH trajectory, lipid panel + ApoB + Lp(a), fasting insulin and A1c, TSH and thyroid antibodies, bone markers, and DHEA-S. You walk into your appointment armed with the complete picture.
Can Lipa identify which perimenopause stage I'm in?
Yes. The STRAW+10 staging framework breaks the menopausal transition into late reproductive, early menopause transition, late menopause transition, early postmenopause, and late postmenopause stages. Lipa cross-references your FSH/estradiol/cycle history against this framework and identifies your stage.
